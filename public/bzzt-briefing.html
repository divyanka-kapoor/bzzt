Executive Briefing

Predicting Disease Outbreaks
Before They Happen

A climate-intelligence system for early warning of dengue, malaria, and chikungunya — operating across 26 countries, free of cost

The Problem

India reports among the highest burdens of dengue, malaria, and chikungunya in the world. Despite decades of investment in disease surveillance, the fundamental challenge remains unchanged: outbreaks are detected after they have already begun. By the time cases reach a district hospital, the mosquito breeding cycle responsible started 10–14 weeks earlier.

Community health workers, district officers, and families at risk receive no advance warning. The information to prevent this exists — in freely available weather data updated daily — but no accessible system converts it into an actionable alert.

The Solution

Bzzt is an automated disease early warning system that predicts outbreak risk for dengue, malaria, and chikungunya at district level — across India and 25 other endemic countries — every night, at no cost.

A machine learning model trained on 1.1 million real outbreak records from 102 countries reads daily climate data for every monitored district and produces a risk score 10 weeks before the outbreak peak. When risk crosses a threshold, an early warning is issued via SMS, WhatsApp, or USSD — a feature phone dial code requiring no internet connection, no smartphone, and no data plan. Alerts are delivered in English and local languages including Hindi, Bengali, and others.

Current Status

Bzzt is a fully operational system — not a prototype. A live intelligence map at bzzt-alerts.vercel.app displays real-time risk across all monitored districts, updated every night by an automated pipeline. India is covered at full district level (676 districts). SMS, WhatsApp, and USSD delivery are integrated and tested. The complete codebase is open-source and available at no cost to any government or institution.

Scientific Validation

The predictive signal has been validated against 2,610 weeks of confirmed dengue surveillance data from FIOCRUZ — Brazil's national disease authority — and cross-validated across 14 cities in 5 countries on 3 continents.

Real-World Confirmation — Delhi, May 2026

Bzzt flagged Delhi as elevated dengue risk based on climate conditions and rising symptom search trends. Delhi's Municipal Corporation subsequently confirmed the highest April dengue case count in five years — 52 cases, up 24% year-on-year — consistent with the Bzzt signal that week.

What a Pilot Involves

A pilot requires no financial commitment and minimal administrative resource from the partner organisation. The system operates automatically.

Coverage Districts within the partner's jurisdiction or area of operation, monitored at no cost
Deliverables Weekly district-level risk reports; early warning alerts for high-risk periods; end-of-pilot summary report
Partner's role Designated point of contact; feedback on alert utility; optional sharing of anonymised case data
Duration Flexible — minimum one transmission season (3–6 months) recommended
Cost No cost to the partner organisation
